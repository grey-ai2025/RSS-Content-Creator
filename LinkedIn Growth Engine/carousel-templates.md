# Carousel Templates — Grey AI LinkedIn

Carousels are Grey AI's highest-performing format (4.6× higher ER than text posts). This file contains slide-by-slide structures for the most proven carousel types.

---

## Grey AI Carousel Brand Guidelines

### Visual Specs
- **Dimensions:** 1080×1350px (4:5 ratio) — optimal for LinkedIn feed
- **Font:** DM Sans (headings: Bold 700, body: Regular 400)
- **Colors:**
  - Background: #0D0D0D (dark) or #FAFAFA (light)
  - Accent: #ADFBF6 (Grey AI cyan)
  - Text on dark: #FFFFFF
  - Text on light: #111111
  - Secondary text: #71717A
- **Logo:** Grey AI logo on slide 1 and last slide
- **Text size:** Headline 48–64px, Body 28–36px, Caption 20–24px
- **Max words per slide:** 25–35 words

### Design Principles
- One idea per slide — never cram
- High contrast for readability
- Generous whitespace (40px+ margins)
- Use the cyan accent for emphasis, not decoration
- Dark backgrounds perform better (higher contrast in feed)

## Template 1: Binary Framework Carousel (Highest ER)

Grey AI's #1 post used this structure. Average ER: 25%+.

### When to Use
- Comparing two approaches, types, or mistakes
- Any "Type 1 vs Type 2" or "Before/After" content
- When you want readers to self-identify

### Slide Structure (6–8 slides)

```
SLIDE 1 — HOOK
[Bold headline that creates tension]
"Same AI tool. Two professionals. Two opposite mistakes."
→ Swipe to find out which one you're making.
[Grey AI logo, small]

SLIDE 2 — SETUP
[Frame the problem]
"Harvard researchers studied 750+ consultants using AI."
"They found two collaboration modes — and most people are stuck in one."
[Simple visual: two icons or split screen]

SLIDE 3 — TYPE 1
[Describe Type 1 clearly]
𝐓𝐲𝐩𝐞 1: The Delegator
"Hands AI everything. Vague prompts, no review, hits publish."
"Feels efficient. Actually dangerous."
[Visual: single color block, icon]

SLIDE 4 — TYPE 1 CONSEQUENCE
[What happens when you're Type 1]
"Output quality drops. Errors multiply. Trust erodes."
"You're not using AI. AI is using you."

SLIDE 5 — TYPE 2
[Describe Type 2 clearly]
𝐓𝐲𝐩𝐞 2: The Avoider
"Won't touch it. 'I don't need AI.' Spends 3 hours on a 15-minute task."
"Feels principled. Actually falling behind."
[Visual: contrasting color block, different icon]

SLIDE 6 — TYPE 2 CONSEQUENCE
[What happens when you're Type 2]
"While you resist, competitors accelerate."
"The gap isn't closing. It's widening."

SLIDE 7 — THE SWEET SPOT
[The answer / resolution]
"The people winning with AI right now?"
"They know when to lead and when to let AI carry."
→ Context in. Review out. Judgment always on.
[Visual: balanced/center element]

SLIDE 8 — CTA
[Question + product mention if relevant]
"Which type are you?"
"🔗 Fix your AI relationship → Link in first comment"
[Grey AI logo + branding]
```

### Caption Template (60–80 words)
```
Same AI tool. Two professionals. Two opposite mistakes.

Swipe to see what Harvard researchers found when they studied 750+ consultants — and the two collaboration modes that separate people who struggle with AI from people who lead with it.

Which mistake do you see more often — people handing AI too much, or not using it enough?

#AILiteracy #PracticalAI #GreyAI
```

## Template 2: Stat Gap Carousel

Uses a surprising statistic to create a knowledge gap. Average ER: 15%+.

### When to Use
- Sharing research findings or industry data
- Revealing a counterintuitive pattern
- When you have a specific, shareable number

### Slide Structure (5–7 slides)

```
SLIDE 1 — THE STAT (hook)
[One big number, impossible to ignore]
"60% usage. 20% delegation."
"That gap is the problem."
[Giant typography for the numbers]

SLIDE 2 — CONTEXT
[Where does this stat come from?]
"Anthropic's 2026 report found that teams are touching AI constantly."
"But handing it real work? Nope."

SLIDE 3 — WHY IT MATTERS
[The implication]
"Rewriting emails ≠ AI adoption."
"Summarizing docs ≠ delegation."
"That's AI as fancy autocomplete."

SLIDE 4 — THE ROOT CAUSE
[Your take on why]
"Nobody taught them how to delegate to AI."
"They taught themselves — which means they stopped at 'this is neat.'"

SLIDE 5 — THE FIX
[Actionable insight]
"The gap between 'using AI' and 'trusting AI with real work'"
"is where most teams are stuck."
→ That's not a technology problem. That's a training problem.

SLIDE 6 — CTA
"Is your team actually using AI — or just poking at it?"
[Grey AI branding + link reference]
```

## Template 3: News-Jack Carousel (Fast Turn)

For reacting to AI news within 24 hours. Prioritize speed over polish.

### When to Use
- Major AI product launch, acquisition, or policy change
- Viral AI story or controversy
- When a stat or quote is trending in the AI space

### Slide Structure (4–6 slides)

```
SLIDE 1 — THE HEADLINE (your take, not the news)
[Not: "OpenAI just released X"]
[Instead: "OpenAI just made every AI training program obsolete."]
[Bold, provocative interpretation]

SLIDE 2 — WHAT HAPPENED
[Brief, factual, 2–3 sentences max]
"Here's what dropped: [feature/announcement]."
"Here's what it means."

SLIDE 3–4 — THE GREY AI TAKE
[Your unique perspective as an AI training company]
"For every team that's excited about this..."
"There's a training gap that just got wider."
[Connect to AI literacy / organizational readiness]

SLIDE 5 — SO WHAT? (for the audience)
[Make it actionable for leaders/teams]
"If your team can't use the tools they already have..."
"Newer tools won't save you."

SLIDE 6 — CTA
[Question related to the news]
"Does this change how you think about AI training?"
[Grey AI branding]
```

## Template 4: Myth-Buster / Contrarian Carousel

For challenging conventional wisdom. Strong for comments and shares.

### Slide Structure (5–7 slides)

```
SLIDE 1 — THE MYTH
[State the common belief boldly]
"Everyone says you need a prompt framework."
"CRISP. RISEN. CO-STAR. RACE."
"They're all overbuilt."

SLIDE 2 — THE EVIDENCE
[Why the myth persists]
"Prompt frameworks are reverse-engineered from good prompts"
"and turned into acronyms so someone can sell a course."

SLIDE 3 — THE REALITY
[Your contrarian take]
"You don't need a framework."
"You need three habits."

SLIDE 4–5 — THE ALTERNATIVE
[Your simpler/better approach]
"→ Tell AI what role to play"
"→ Give it the specific context"
"→ Ask one clear question at a time"
"That's it. That's the whole framework."

SLIDE 6 — THE CLOSER
"If your prompt is longer than the answer you want,"
"you're overcomplicating it."
"Clear beats clever. Every time."

SLIDE 7 — CTA
"Do you use a prompt framework — or just write clearly?"
[Grey AI branding]
```

## Carousel Creation Workflow

### Option A: Design Tool (Recommended)
1. Open Canva → LinkedIn Carousel template (1080×1350)
2. Apply Grey AI brand colors (#0D0D0D background, #ADFBF6 accent, DM Sans font)
3. Build slides following the template structure above
4. Export as PDF (this is what LinkedIn uses for carousel/document posts)
5. Upload to LinkedIn as a "Document" post

### Option B: Code-Generated (For Claude)
When the user asks Claude to create a carousel:
1. Generate an HTML file with individual slide designs
2. Use Grey AI brand guidelines (colors, fonts, spacing)
3. Each slide = one "page" element at 1080×1350px
4. Export/screenshot individual slides or convert to PDF
5. Deliver as a single PDF file ready for LinkedIn upload

### Option C: Quick Text Carousel
For speed (news-jacking), create a simple text-on-background carousel:
1. Dark background (#0D0D0D)
2. White text, DM Sans Bold
3. One sentence per slide, large font
4. Cyan accent for emphasis words
5. Minimal design, maximum readability

## Carousel Checklist

Before publishing any carousel:

- [ ] 5–8 slides (not fewer, not more than 10)
- [ ] Slide 1 hooks — would you stop scrolling?
- [ ] One idea per slide — no cramming
- [ ] Max 35 words per slide
- [ ] Grey AI branding (logo on first and last slide)
- [ ] Dark background for feed contrast
- [ ] Caption under 100 words
- [ ] Caption ends with a question
- [ ] No link in caption — "link in first comment" only
- [ ] Exported as PDF for LinkedIn document upload
