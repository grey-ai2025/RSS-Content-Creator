Venture capital has a new philosophy: why pick a side when you can hedge the entire AI arms race?

A TechCrunch investigation found that at least 12 VC firms — including Founders Fund and ICONIQ Capital — have invested in both OpenAI and Anthropic. Direct competitors. Same sector. Same board-level access. The piece describes it as "the disregard of a longstanding ethical conflict-of-interest rule," and some of the dual investments are described plainly as "shocking."

The old rule was simple: if you're on a company's board, you don't fund their competition. You have too much access to confidential strategy, pricing, and talent decisions. It's not just bad optics — it's a real governance problem.

But the AI boom has apparently rewritten the rules. When capital is abundant and nobody knows which lab will "win," portfolio hedging looks rational. The implicit message from these 12+ firms: we don't know if it's going to be OpenAI or Anthropic, and we'd like exposure either way, thank you.

For founders raising from VCs with dual AI positions: ask the question directly. Who else in this exact space are they backing?

For limited partners in those funds: same question.

The AI supercycle is extraordinary enough that even experienced investors are abandoning frameworks they've held for decades. Whether that's pragmatic or a red flag probably depends on whose board meeting gets compromised first.
