Someone built 24,000 fake accounts and sent 16 million queries to extract a frontier AI model's capabilities. That is not scraping. That is an industrial operation.

Anthropic documented the campaign in detail: a coordinated effort by Chinese AI labs — DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax — to systematically capture Claude's outputs and use them to train competing models. Model distillation at this scale means the "student" model learns from the "teacher" model's responses without the teacher's consent, compensation, or knowledge.

Separately, DeepSeek reportedly trained its latest model on Nvidia Blackwell chips subject to U.S. export controls. The two stories together are not coincidental.

**The IP chain-of-custody question is now unavoidable.** If a cheaper AI model was trained on systematically extracted outputs from a premium model, what are the legal and ethical implications of deploying it inside an enterprise? This is not a theoretical question. It is a procurement and compliance question — and most organizations don't have a framework for it yet.

**AI governance is now a commercial risk, not just a policy concern.** Governance frameworks built around model transparency, access controls, and output provenance are lagging behind the actual threat landscape. The combination of capability extraction and export control violations signals that the AI race has geopolitical stakes that flow directly into enterprise vendor selection.

**Disclosure itself is the notable move.** Most labs do not publicly document security breaches or IP extraction at this scale. The fact that this was surfaced changes the industry conversation — and raises the expectation that others will have to account for similar exposures.

The question is no longer whether your AI vendors are secure. It is whether you've asked them. 🔐
