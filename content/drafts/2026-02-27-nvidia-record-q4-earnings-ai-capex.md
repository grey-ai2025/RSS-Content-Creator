$2.5 trillion is being spent on AI infrastructure in 2026. That number exceeds the combined inflation-adjusted cost of the Apollo and Manhattan projects.

And only 8% of Americans say they'd pay extra for it.

Nvidia just posted ~$43 billion in quarterly profit — nearly double the prior year. Jensen Huang described demand for compute as having "gone completely exponential." The hyperscalers are not slowing down. The infrastructure bet is getting bigger.

The counter-signals are just as loud.

**80% of executives** across a 6,000-firm survey report AI has had zero measurable impact on jobs or productivity. The ROI gap between what's being built and what's being demonstrated is not a rounding error — it is the central question of the AI economy right now.

This is not a contradiction. It is a timing gap.

Infrastructure precedes productivity. The electrical grid was built before most factories were rewired to use it. The internet backbone was laid before e-commerce was a coherent concept. The firms winning the AI economy in 2028 are almost certainly over-investing in infrastructure in 2026 — and the ones waiting for proof of ROI before committing are running out of time to catch up.

The real question is not whether $2.5 trillion is too much. It is whether your organization is positioned to capture value when the infrastructure pays off — or just watching someone else's capex become someone else's competitive advantage. 📊
