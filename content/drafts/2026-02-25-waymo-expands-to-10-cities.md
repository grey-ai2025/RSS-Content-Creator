The robots are coming for your Uber surge pricing. And honestly? Fair.

Waymo just dropped its robotaxi service into four new US cities simultaneously — Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, and Orlando — bringing its total footprint to 10 cities. That's not a pilot anymore. That's a rollout strategy.

Each vehicle packs 29 cameras, five lidars, and six radar trackers providing 360-degree environmental awareness. Your average Uber driver has two eyes and a phone mount. The hardware gap is not subtle.

The Sun Belt expansion is a deliberate play. Grid-based streets, favorable weather, and high car-dependency make these cities close to ideal operating conditions for autonomous vehicles. Waymo isn't going where it's hardest first — it's going where it can scale fastest. Classic Geoffrey Moore, except the chasm is "roads without unexpected left turns."

For transportation and logistics professionals, 10 cities is the moment you start updating your forecasts. For Uber and Lyft: two companies that have been investing in autonomous solutions for years, now watching a competitor actually deploy them at scale, in their backyard.

Access is still invite-only via the Waymo app. So the robots are here — they're just being polite about it for now.
