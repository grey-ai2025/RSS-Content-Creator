12% of U.S. teenagers are using AI chatbots for emotional support. The tools they are using were not designed for that.

A Pew Research survey reveals how embedded AI has become in adolescent daily life — not just for productivity but for social and emotional roles. 57% use it to search for information. 54% use it for schoolwork. 16% use it for casual conversation. And 12% use it for emotional support or advice.

ChatGPT, Claude, and Grok are general-purpose tools. None of them are designed for therapeutic use. And yet the design gap between "what this tool was built for" and "what people are actually using it for" is now 12% of an entire generation.

The perception gap compounds the problem.

64% of teens self-report using AI chatbots. Only 51% of their parents believe they do — a 13-point gap. Only 18% of parents approve of emotional support use. That means most of this use is happening outside any adult oversight or structured guidance.

The implications extend beyond adolescence.

OpenAI retired a version of GPT-4o after user backlash over emotional dependency in adults. Character.AI disabled features for under-18 users following legal action after two teen suicides. These are not edge cases — they are signals about what happens when AI literacy is absent and default human behavior fills the void.

The next generation of workers is forming its baseline understanding of what AI is — and what it is for — from unsupervised personal use. Not from training programs. Not from employer guidance. From whatever the product allows.

AI literacy is the new baseline. The question is who teaches it — and when.
