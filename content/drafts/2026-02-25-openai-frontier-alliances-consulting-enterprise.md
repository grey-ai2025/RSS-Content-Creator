The world's most prominent AI lab just hired four of the world's largest consulting firms to help sell its product.

BCG. McKinsey. Accenture. Capgemini. All in.

That is not a marketing move. It's an admission.

The admission: enterprise AI transformation is not a technology problem. It's a human problem. And no AI model — however capable — has figured out how to solve misaligned incentives, legacy process debt, and organizational change resistance on its own.

BCG's own CEO put it plainly: "AI alone does not drive transformation. It must be linked to strategy, built into redesigned processes, and adopted at scale with aligned incentives and culture."

Hard to argue with that. Especially when the COO of the same AI company simultaneously acknowledged that enterprise AI hasn't actually penetrated core business processes yet — despite $20 billion in annualized revenue and years of enterprise sales motion.

There's a structural shift buried in this partnership that most people are glossing over: success is being measured by business outcomes, not seat licenses.

That single change in metric is more disruptive to the traditional SaaS model than any product feature. It means AI vendors are now accountable for whether the thing actually works inside your organization — not just whether your employees have access to it.

For enterprise leaders, the signal is clear: your next AI transformation project is going to arrive through a consulting partner's door, packaged with multi-year implementation commitments and process redesign scope.

Is your organization evaluating AI investments on the right metrics yet?
