The pilot budget for AI agents just ran out.

A survey of 200 finance leaders across the U.S., UK, France, and Germany found that 61% had deployed AI agents only as experiments. One in four executives admitted they don't know the ROI.

That was 2025. The framing in 2026 is different.

In a single week: Anthropic admitted 2025 was "a failure of approach" and launched production-grade enterprise agents. OpenAI shipped its Frontier platform for enterprise agent management. Atlassian gave agents their own task queues in Jira. These are not coincidental timing. This is an industry-wide pivot — from "show what's possible" to "prove what it's worth."

The shift from experimentation to production rewrites every conversation happening inside enterprise AI teams right now.

**Pilot logic:** Does this work? Can we demo it?
**Production logic:** What are the KPIs? Who owns the outcome? How do we govern it when it fails?

Those are harder questions. They require infrastructure, accountability frameworks, and integration with operational budgets — not innovation budgets.

The companies that ran rigorous pilots are now ahead. The ones that treated pilots as checkbox exercises have a narrowing window to catch up. And the AI vendors who spent 2025 selling access now have to deliver outcomes — because the budget conversation has moved from "exploring AI" to "measuring AI."

Always-on AI was the promise. Production deployment is the proof of concept.

2026 is where the gap between hype and production gets priced in.
