24,000 fake accounts. 16 million engineered conversations. All designed to steal the most advanced capabilities of a frontier AI model.

This isn't a cybersecurity thought experiment. It happened. It was documented. And it tells us something important about where competitive value in AI actually lives.

The technique is called model distillation — a legitimate AI training method where a smaller model learns from the outputs of a larger one. When you do it to your own models, it's efficient engineering. When you do it to a competitor's model at scale using fraudulent accounts, it's IP theft.

What's striking about the attacks is what was specifically targeted: agentic reasoning, tool use, and coding capabilities. Not general knowledge. Not language fluency. The attackers went straight for the most differentiated, hardest-to-replicate functions in the model.

That tells us something about the competitive map of frontier AI right now. The real moat isn't a model that can write a decent paragraph. It's one that can reason through multi-step tasks, use tools autonomously, and execute complex workflows without hand-holding. That's where the value is concentrated — and that's exactly where the extraction was focused.

There's a second-order risk here that deserves attention. Models built through illicit distillation don't inherit the safety constraints of the original. Dangerous capabilities — the kind that require careful safeguards around bioweapons synthesis or cyberattack generation — can propagate without any of the guardrails that were built to contain them.

The AI governance conversation typically focuses on how organizations use AI internally. This story reframes the question: how are you protecting your AI investment from external extraction?
