$100 billion. For chips. From a single deal.

If that number doesn't recalibrate your sense of the scale of this AI infrastructure buildout, let's add some context: the chips being purchased will support roughly six gigawatts of data center power demand. That's enough electricity to power approximately 4.5 million homes.

The AI arms race is no longer primarily a software competition. It's a physical infrastructure one.

What makes this deal particularly interesting strategically is the structure. AMD issued a warrant for up to 10% of its own company — at a penny per share — in exchange for the purchase commitment. Equity for chips. It's a financing model that OpenAI and AMD pioneered last October, and it's rapidly becoming the defining transaction structure of the AI era.

This is what enterprise procurement looks like at the frontier: your technology vendor takes a stake in your company's success, and you take a stake in theirs. It's not a software subscription. It's a strategic alliance with equity on both sides of the table.

The underlying strategic driver is diversification away from Nvidia's pricing power — and frankly, away from the single-supplier dependency risk that has quietly become a board-level concern across major technology organizations.

AMD CEO Lisa Su said the CPU market is "absolutely on fire" as agentic AI scales and inference workloads grow. That tells you where the real compute demand is shifting: not just training massive models, but running millions of agents continuously.

The next constraint on AI at enterprise scale might not be model quality. It might be kilowatts.
