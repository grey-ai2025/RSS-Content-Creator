Hot take from the COO of the world's leading AI company: enterprise AI hasn't actually penetrated core business processes yet.

Not a critic. Not a skeptic. The COO. Of the company at the center of every enterprise AI pitch deck for the last three years.

That's either the most honest thing said at a major AI summit this year, or the most alarming, depending on how you look at it.

The gap they're describing is real and I've watched it play out repeatedly. Individual productivity tools? Genuinely useful, widely adopted. Getting AI to operate inside the messy, multi-team, legacy-tool-dependent reality of an actual enterprise workflow? Fundamentally different problem. Still unsolved at scale.

What's changing is the measurement model. The enterprise agent platform launching now will be evaluated on business outcomes — not seat licenses. That single shift in metric matters more than most people realize.

Seat licenses measure access. Business outcomes measure impact. Tying revenue to the latter means AI vendors are now accountable for whether the transformation actually happens — not just whether you paid for the subscription.

For enterprise leaders, this is your signal to recalibrate. If your AI strategy currently lives in individual tool adoption and productivity dashboards, you're measuring the wrong thing.

The question isn't "how many of our employees use AI?" It's "which core business processes run differently because of it?"

If you can't answer the second question, your AI investment is still in the pilot phase — even if it doesn't feel like it.

What business outcome would make you confident your AI investment is actually working?
