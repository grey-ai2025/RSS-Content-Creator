Your autonomous humanoid robot may include one carefully hidden human.

MIT Technology Review dropped an investigation this week into how companies developing humanoid robots systematically downplay — or outright conceal — the extent to which remote human teleoperators are essential to making those robots function. The polished demos showing robots autonomously navigating complex tasks? Often, there's a person at a joystick somewhere making that happen in real time.

It's the Wizard of Oz, but with better venture funding.

Here's what most people are missing: this isn't just a PR problem. It has real implications for investors valuing these companies at multi-billion-dollar autonomous-capability premiums, enterprise buyers negotiating robotics contracts, and regulators assessing labor displacement impact.

If "automation" is partly human labor wearing a robot costume, the economics look very different.

The parallels to AI more broadly are uncomfortable but important. Ghost work — hidden human labor propping up the appearance of full automation — has been a recurring pattern throughout tech history. The humanoid robot space may be the latest chapter.

The future of work conversation usually focuses on what AI and robots will replace. This story asks a harder question: how much of the "replacement" narrative is actually accurate right now, versus how much is a capability story being told prematurely to keep the hype — and the funding rounds — alive?

We are at an inflection point where responsible AI at scale requires honesty about what these systems can actually do. The hype cycle eventually ends. Transparency is a better long-term bet.
