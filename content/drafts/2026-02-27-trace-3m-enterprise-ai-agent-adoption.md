The agents work in demos. They stall in production. Someone just raised $3M to fix that second part.

Trace — backed by Y Combinator — is building tooling for the layer that sits between a capable AI agent and a functioning enterprise workflow. Not the model. Not the interface. The implementation and change management infrastructure that determines whether the capability ever gets used at all.

80% of executives in a 6,000-firm survey report AI has had zero measurable impact on jobs or productivity. That number doesn't mean AI doesn't work. It means deployment doesn't work.

**The capability gap is closing fast. The adoption gap is not.** Labs are shipping more capable agents every quarter. Enterprise deployments are still stuck in pilot purgatory — held back by integration complexity, compliance requirements, and organizations built around workflows that weren't designed for AI inputs. The gap between those two trajectories is the business problem Trace is betting on.

**From experimentation to production is not a technology step.** It is a change management step. The AI tooling ecosystem has invested almost entirely in building better models and better interfaces. Almost nothing has been built for the human systems — approval chains, training gaps, incentive misalignments — that determine whether a capable agent actually gets deployed and trusted.

**The adoption layer is the underbuilt layer.** For every dollar invested in AI agent capability, a fraction goes into the infrastructure for organizational readiness. YC's backing of Trace signals that the smartest early-stage investors see the deployment gap as a durable problem — one that persists regardless of which model wins.

AI-first strategy doesn't start with the model. It starts with the organization ready to use it.
