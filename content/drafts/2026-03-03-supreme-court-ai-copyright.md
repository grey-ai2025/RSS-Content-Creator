## HEADLINE
The US Supreme Court has let stand a federal ruling that AI-generated content cannot be copyrighted — making human authorship a legal requirement for IP ownership.

## KEY FACTS
- "Human authorship is a bedrock requirement of copyright" — federal courts have now affirmed this at every level, including appeals court in 2025
- The Copyright Office confirmed separately: AI artwork generated from text prompts is not copyright-eligible
- The US Patent Office issued parallel guidance: AI systems cannot be listed as inventors on patents
- Source credibility: US Supreme Court, US District Court, US Copyright Office, US Patent Office — all branches of US IP governance aligned

## GREY AI ANGLE
Any organization using generative AI to produce creative assets — brand imagery, marketing copy, code — is now operating in a defined legal landscape: AI-generated output without substantial human authorship cannot be owned. This is an AI literacy issue hiding inside a legal story. Teams building agentic content pipelines need to understand where human creative input must exist to create protectable work.

## RAW MATERIAL
US District Court Judge Beryl Howell, August 2023: "Human authorship is a bedrock requirement of copyright." The Copyright Office: AI artwork based on text prompts is not copyright-eligible. US Patent Office: AI systems cannot be listed as inventors, though humans may use AI in development. The legal gap: "using AI as a tool" (copyrightable with human creative input) versus "AI generating independently" (not copyrightable) — a distinction that grows more complex as agentic AI operates with less direct human oversight.
