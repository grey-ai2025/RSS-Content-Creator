# US Tells Diplomats to Lobby Against Foreign Data Sovereignty Laws

**Source URL:** https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/25/us-tells-diplomats-to-lobby-against-foreign-data-sovereignty-laws/

**Publication Date:** February 25, 2026

**Keyword Match:** AI governance and security, AI in the enterprise, open source AI models

---

## Summary

The Trump administration has formally ordered U.S. diplomats to lobby against foreign data sovereignty and data localization laws, arguing they threaten the global expansion of American AI services. A diplomatic cable signed by Secretary of State Marco Rubio instructs diplomats to "counter unnecessarily burdensome regulations" including data localization mandates, and to track proposals promoting data sovereignty. The directive explicitly identifies the EU's GDPR, Digital Services Act, and AI Act as the type of regulation the U.S. opposes. The order puts U.S. diplomacy directly in service of American AI companies' commercial interests — and places it in direct conflict with global regulatory trends.

---

## Key Data Points

- Cable signed by: U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio
- Key argument in cable: Data sovereignty laws would "disrupt global data flows, increase costs and cybersecurity risks, limit AI and cloud services, and expand government control in ways that can undermine civil liberties and enable censorship"
- Directive instructs diplomats to: track proposals promoting data sovereignty laws; promote the Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum
- The administration explicitly targets: EU GDPR, Digital Services Act, AI Act
- The Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum is an international body promoting "trusted data flows globally through international data protection and privacy certifications"
- The order is consistent with the Trump administration's broader opposition to international AI and tech regulation
- State Department did not return requests for comment at time of publication

---

## Why It Matters

U.S. foreign policy is now being used as a competitive instrument for American AI companies. This has direct implications for multinational enterprises operating across EU and APAC jurisdictions, where data residency and sovereignty requirements are tightening. For professional audiences in compliance, legal, and enterprise AI, the story signals a fundamental geopolitical fault line: the U.S. is positioning AI data governance as a trade issue, while the EU and others are positioning it as a civil liberties issue. Companies operating across these jurisdictions will face increasing pressure to choose sides — or build infrastructure that satisfies both. This is a direct challenge to the EU AI Act's 2026 enforcement deadlines.
