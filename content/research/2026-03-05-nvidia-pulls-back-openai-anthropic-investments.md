# Nvidia Is Pulling Back From OpenAI and Anthropic Investments — And the Real Reason May Be Messier Than Jensen Huang Admits

## Summary

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang confirmed at the Morgan Stanley Technology, Media and Telecom conference that his company's investments in OpenAI and Anthropic are likely its last — stating that once both companies go public (expected later this year), the investment window closes. The stated rationale is plausible on its surface: late-stage private investing becomes less attractive near IPO. But TechCrunch's analysis surfaces a more complicated reality: Nvidia's investments in both companies have become strategically awkward in ways that the IPO explanation doesn't fully account for.

The circular nature of AI investment is a core tension. When Nvidia announced a $100 billion investment commitment in OpenAI last September, an MIT Sloan professor characterized it as "kind of a wash" — OpenAI would use the capital to buy Nvidia chips, and Nvidia would gain a stake in its own customer. The investment Nvidia ultimately finalized in OpenAI's $110 billion round came in at $30 billion — well short of the original pledge. The gap between the announcement and the execution is notable. Meanwhile, the Anthropic relationship has become overtly complicated: Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei publicly compared Nvidia's practice of selling high-performance AI chips to approved Chinese customers to "selling nuclear weapons to North Korea" at Davos in January 2026. The Trump administration's subsequent blacklisting of Anthropic put Nvidia in the position of holding equity in a company now designated as a federal supply-chain risk.

The commercial irony is sharp: Nvidia is the dominant chip supplier to both OpenAI and Anthropic, generating billions in revenue from each. Adding equity stakes in companies that are now geopolitically and competitively antagonistic to each other — and to the US government's preferred posture — is a complexity that a clean "IPO window" narrative doesn't capture.

## Relevance

This story captures a structural risk that is underappreciated in enterprise AI discussions: the AI infrastructure investment stack is increasingly circular and politically exposed. Nvidia sells chips to companies it owns equity in. Those companies are now taking opposite sides of a politically charged government contract dispute. The company supplying the infrastructure to the entire AI industry is now disentangling from the ecosystem it helped build, for reasons that may have as much to do with geopolitical positioning as portfolio returns. For enterprise leaders, the signal is: the AI chip supply chain is not a neutral utility — it is a politically contested arena, and the winners of the AI governance debate will shape who gets access to compute.
