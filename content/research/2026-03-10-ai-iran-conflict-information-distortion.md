# AI Is Turning the Iran War Into Real-Time Theater — and Making It Harder to Understand

**Title:** How AI is turning the Iran conflict into theater

**Source URL:** https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/03/09/1134063/how-ai-is-turning-the-iran-conflict-into-theater/

**Publication Date:** March 9, 2026

**Keyword Match:** AI safety and military ethics; AI governance and sovereignty; AI cyber risk

**Summary:** MIT Technology Review reports on a new phenomenon emerging from the US-Israel strikes against Iran: a proliferation of AI-powered "intelligence dashboards" built in days using vibe coding tools, offering real-time satellite imagery, ship tracking, AI-generated news summaries, and links to prediction markets betting on outcomes like Iran's next supreme leader. While proponents claim these tools democratize access to intelligence-grade information, digital investigations experts warn they create an "illusion of understanding" — assembling raw signals without the expertise to interpret them. The AI-generated summaries on these dashboards introduce inaccuracies, the data is uncurated, and fake AI-generated satellite imagery is already spreading online. Meanwhile, Palantir is confirmed as the platform through which the US military accesses AI models like Claude in the conflict, deepening the entanglement between commercial AI and active warfare.

**Key Data Points:**
- A dashboard built by two Andreessen Horowitz staffers combines open-source satellite data, ship tracking, news feeds, and Kalshi prediction market links in real time
- Digital investigations expert Craig Silverman has logged over 20 such AI-powered wartime intelligence dashboards
- Many dashboards were "vibe-coded" in a matter of days using AI tools
- Palantir confirmed as the platform through which the US military accesses AI models including Claude in the Iran conflict
- Prediction market Polymarket removed betting on nuclear detonation; Kalshi offered bets on Iran's next supreme leader (Mojtaba Khamenei was selected, per reports)
- Financial Times found AI-generated fake satellite imagery of the Iran conflict spreading online
- Silverman quote: "There's an illusion of being on top of things and being in control, where all you're really doing is pulling in a ton of signals and not necessarily understanding what you're seeing"
- The US military continues to use Anthropic's Claude in the conflict despite the supply-chain risk designation
- AI-generated content makes it cheaper and faster to spread fake imagery — eroding trust in satellite evidence, which is typically considered highly reliable

**Why It Matters:** This story captures a new and underappreciated dimension of AI's governance problem: not just the risks of AI in weapons systems, but AI's role in degrading the information environment that democratic publics rely on to make sense of war. The same capabilities that make AI useful — rapid synthesis, accessibility, low barriers to deployment — are being used to manufacture the feeling of informed understanding without the substance. For professional audiences, this is a case study in what happens when AI tools designed for productivity are repurposed for information warfare. It also illustrates how AI governance failures aren't always about autonomous weapons — sometimes they're about the softer, more pervasive erosion of epistemic trust.
