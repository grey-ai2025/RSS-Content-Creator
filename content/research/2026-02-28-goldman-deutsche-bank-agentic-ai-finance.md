# Goldman Sachs and Deutsche Bank Test Agentic AI for Real-Time Trade Surveillance

## Summary

Goldman Sachs and Deutsche Bank are actively testing agentic AI systems for real-time trade surveillance — a move that would replace static, rules-based compliance monitoring with adaptive agents that reason across multiple data streams simultaneously. Deutsche Bank is partnering with Google Cloud to deploy agents that monitor large datasets of order and execution data in near real-time, scanning for "complex anomalies" by examining the relationships between trades, timing, market conditions, and individual trader history. Goldman Sachs is independently exploring agentic AI systems capable of "a degree of independence in scanning for misconduct indicators" — flagging patterns that don't fit predefined rules but still appear unusual. In both cases, human compliance officers remain responsible for reviewing flagged cases; the AI functions as an enhanced detection layer, not a decision-maker. The approach moves beyond traditional keyword and threshold scanning toward genuine pattern reasoning.

## Key Data Points

- Goldman Sachs: exploring agentic AI that identifies misconduct patterns without clear predefined rules
- Deutsche Bank: partnering with Google Cloud for near-real-time trade surveillance agents
- Deutsche Bank's system analyzes: order and execution data, relationships between trades, timing, market conditions, trader history
- Traditional surveillance: relies on preset rules and keyword alerts
- New approach: multi-signal pattern recognition across structured and unstructured data streams
- Human role preserved: compliance officers review all flagged cases; AI does not make determinations
- Context: Bloomberg reported on these pilots as part of broader industry exploration
- Industry pressure: FT Longitude survey found 61% of finance leaders have deployed AI agents only as experiments, with 1 in 4 executives admitting no clear ROI measurement

## Why It Matters

Financial services is the most compliance-heavy enterprise sector — and it is now running live experiments with AI agents in the most sensitive part of its operations: misconduct detection. This signals that agentic AI has crossed from experimentation into serious institutional deployment in regulated environments. The choice to keep humans in the loop for final determinations (while expanding what AI can autonomously scan and flag) is a template for how high-stakes industries will govern agentic AI: extend the AI's detection surface, compress human review to judgment calls only. For enterprise professionals outside finance, the Goldman/Deutsche Bank approach is a preview of how AI agent governance gets operationalized in regulated environments — a model that will migrate across sectors.
