# The Human Work Behind Humanoid Robots Is Being Hidden

**Title:** The Human Work Behind Humanoid Robots Is Being Hidden

**Source URL:** https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/02/23/1133508/the-human-work-behind-humanoid-robots-is-being-hidden/

**Publication Date:** February 23, 2026

**Summary:**
MIT Technology Review investigates how companies developing humanoid robots systematically downplay or conceal the degree to which human workers — often remote teleoperators — are essential to making those robots function. Public demonstrations and marketing materials frequently present these robots as autonomous systems, when in practice they require significant real-time human guidance to complete complex tasks. The investigation raises serious questions about transparency in AI and robotics development, the misleading nature of capability claims, and the impact on workers whose labor makes these technologies appear more advanced than they actually are. The piece draws parallels to earlier controversies in AI, where "behind the curtain" human labor has been systematically rendered invisible in favor of a fully automated narrative.

**Key Data Points:**
- Many humanoid robot demonstrations rely on remote human operators controlling robot actions in real time
- Robot companies rarely disclose the extent of human teleoperation in public demos or marketing
- The practice creates a misleading impression of autonomous capability for investors, media, and regulators
- The article draws parallels to "ghost work" — the hidden human labor that supports AI and tech platforms
- Published by MIT Technology Review on February 23, 2026
- The story comes as humanoid robot companies including Figure, Boston Dynamics, Agility Robotics, and others compete aggressively for investment and enterprise contracts
- Infosys chairman Nandan Nilekani and others have noted AI has made automation narratives easier to sell — but not always more accurate

**Why It Matters:**
This story is a critical check on one of the most hyped segments of the AI market. If humanoid robots are not yet capable of genuine autonomy and companies are obscuring that fact, it has profound implications for: investors valuing these companies at multi-billion-dollar valuations; enterprise buyers considering robotics contracts; and regulators assessing the labor displacement impact of automation. For professional audiences — particularly those in operations, manufacturing, supply chain, and HR — this piece cuts through the hype and asks the right question: how much of this "automation" is actually human labor wearing a robot costume? That's a question every technology leader should be asking.
