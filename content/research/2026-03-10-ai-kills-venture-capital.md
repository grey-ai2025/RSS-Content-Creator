## Summary
Wired's investigation into AI-powered venture capital reveals that autonomous deal-investing networks are already making real investment decisions — not as experiments, but with actual capital. ADIN (Autonomous Deal Investing Network), launched in 2025, uses approximately a dozen AI agents with distinct investing personas to evaluate pitch decks, conduct diligence, flag regulatory risks, and recommend deal allocations in about an hour — compared to days or weeks for human analysts. The platform has already invested $100,000 in a real seed round with no human involvement in the diligence process. The deeper existential threat VCs are quietly discussing isn't that AI will replace investors — it's that AI is making startups so cheap to build that fewer of them will need venture capital at all. Midjourney, with 100 employees and $300M+ in annual revenue, took zero VC money.

## Key Data Points
- ADIN uses ~12 distinct AI agent personas (Tech Oracle, Unit Master, Monopoly Maker, etc.) to evaluate startups
- ADIN completed a full diligence process and invested $100,000 in a real seed deal with no human lead on diligence
- Human analysts typically take days to weeks; ADIN completes the equivalent process in approximately one hour
- VCs invested more than $200 billion into AI last year — the most bullish sector in VC history
- Vinod Khosla predicts AI will replace 80% of job responsibilities by 2030
- Midjourney: ~100 employees, $300M+ annual revenue, raised zero VC funding
- The average software unicorn raised $370 million (SaaStr data); that model is now under pressure
- Marc Andreessen argues VC is "one of the last remaining fields" that AI won't fully replace because it's "art, not science"
- Angel Square's Brian Nichols: "The existential threat is: the money's there, but the need from the founder isn't"
- Hustle Fund partner used Claude Code to build a tool to triage founder emails

## Why It Matters
VC is the sector that funds AI — and now AI is both automating VC's core work and eliminating the conditions that made VC necessary. This isn't a speculative future scenario; ADIN has already cut real checks. The argument that VC requires "human taste" mirrors the arguments made by every profession AI has since disrupted. For enterprise leaders, the more immediate signal is structural: if software companies can be built for under six figures with AI-assisted development, the entire capital formation model for B2B software is changing. The funding rounds, valuations, and revenue multiples that define the enterprise software market were calibrated to a world where startups needed millions to hire engineers. That world is ending.
