# AI Industry Pours $125M Into Super PAC to Stop Pro-Regulation Candidates — A Former Tech Exec Is in Their Crosshairs

## Summary

A Silicon Valley-backed super PAC called "Leading the Future" has raised $125 million with an explicit mandate: defeat state-level candidates who support AI regulation and elect those who favor a "light-to-no-touch" approach. Its first primary target is Alex Bores, a New York Assembly member and former Palantir employee who sponsored the RAISE Act — an AI transparency law signed in December 2025 that requires large AI labs to maintain and publish safety plans and report catastrophic safety incidents. The PAC is backed by Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, OpenAI President Greg Brockman, Andreessen Horowitz, and Perplexity.

The irony is structural: Bores is being attacked for AI regulation by the very sector he worked in. He left Palantir in 2019 specifically over its ICE contracts. The RAISE Act he sponsored is described even by critics as unusually light-touch — it covers only AI labs with $500M+ in revenue, requires disclosure and safety planning rather than proactive oversight, and has no prohibition on any specific AI use. Despite this, the PAC has committed to spending at least $10 million against Bores' race — extraordinary spending for a state assembly district where typical total campaign spending is under $100,000.

Meta is also deploying $65 million into two separate super PACs targeting state-level candidates friendly to the tech industry. AI companies and executives collectively donated $83 million to federal campaigns and committees in 2025. The counter-force backing Bores is an Anthropic-funded group, Public First Action, spending $450,000 — a 22:1 spending disadvantage. This is now the architecture of AI governance: not legislation, but capture of the legislators.

- Bores would be only the second Democrat in Congress with a computer science degree if elected
- A separate Bores proposal would require training data disclosure and metadata standards for synthetic content traceability

## Relevance

The story reframes the AI governance debate. The conventional narrative is that AI regulation is stalled because legislators don't understand the technology. The actual picture is more direct: the industry is spending at unprecedented scale to remove legislators who do understand it and want guardrails. For LinkedIn's professional audience, this is the context for every enterprise AI policy discussion happening right now — the political environment around AI governance is not neutral, it is being actively shaped by massive capital deployment. Enterprise leaders building AI governance frameworks need to understand that the regulatory landscape is a contested political artifact, not a technical inevitability.
