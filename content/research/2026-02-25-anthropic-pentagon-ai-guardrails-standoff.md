# Summary

A high-stakes standoff between a leading AI lab and the U.S. Department of Defense has escalated to an ultimatum: agree to terms allowing the military unrestricted "any lawful use" of its AI model, or face being designated a "supply chain risk" — a classification normally reserved for foreign adversaries. The Pentagon's chief technology officer, a former Uber executive, has been driving a pressure campaign that includes threats to invoke the Defense Production Act to compel compliance, a legal tool last used during the COVID-19 pandemic to force manufacturers to produce medical equipment.

The crux of the dispute comes down to two narrow restrictions the AI company refuses to remove: the prohibition on its models being used for fully autonomous lethal weapons systems (with no human in the decision-making loop) and the prohibition on mass domestic surveillance. The company argues both positions reflect not only its own ethical commitments but existing U.S. government directives — including Department of Defense directives on human oversight of lethal force and statutory restrictions on domestic intelligence collection. Its rivals, including OpenAI, xAI, and Google, have reportedly already agreed to the Pentagon's "any lawful use" language in their own contracts.

The stakes are existential for the AI lab in a way that goes beyond its $200 million Pentagon contract. Because its model is the only frontier AI system cleared to operate on fully classified Pentagon networks — sitting inside critical workflows run through major defense platforms — a "supply chain risk" designation would require every defense contractor hoping to do government business to certify they have removed the company's AI from all their systems. That ripple effect could devastate the company's broader commercial business and raise serious questions for investors about the stability of U.S. tech policy.

# Key Data Points

- The AI lab's valuation stands at approximately $380 billion
- The direct Pentagon contract at stake is worth $200 million
- Its AI model is the only frontier model with Impact Level 6 (IL6) classified security clearance, deployed through major defense infrastructure
- OpenAI, xAI, and Google have already agreed to the Pentagon's new "any lawful use" contract terms
- The Pentagon signed a separate agreement with a rival AI company to use its model in classified systems, though that model lacks IL6 clearance
- The Defense Production Act, which the government has threatened to invoke, was last used during the COVID-19 pandemic to compel General Motors and 3M to manufacture medical equipment
- The specific red lines the AI company refuses to cross: (1) fully autonomous kinetic weapons with no human in the loop, and (2) mass domestic surveillance of American citizens
- The Pentagon's January AI strategy memo states the department must "utilize models free from usage policy constraints that may limit lawful military applications"
- Existing DoD Directive 3000.09 already requires human judgment over use of lethal force; the company argues its policy simply enforces the government's own stated principles
