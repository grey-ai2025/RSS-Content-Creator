# Research: Scaling Intelligent Automation Without Breaking Live Workflows

**Title:** Scaling intelligent automation without breaking live workflows

**Publication Date:** March 6, 2026

**Keyword Match:** AI governance and enterprise AI strategy (#9); AI agents — enterprise adoption (#2)

---

## Summary

At the Intelligent Automation Conference, enterprise practitioners from Royal Mail, NatWest Group, Air Liquide, and AXA XL laid out why most automation scaling initiatives stall — and what the ones that succeed actually do differently. The core argument, articulated by Promise Akwaowo (Process Automation Analyst at Royal Mail), is that sustainable automation at scale requires architectural elasticity, not simply deploying more bots. Companies that treat automation as a numbers game — adding volume without building resilient infrastructure — end up with fragile, high-maintenance systems rather than scalable platforms.

The discussion surfaced a widely shared enterprise AI failure mode: large-scale immediate deployments that skip the phased validation approach. The practitioners argued that progress "must be gradual, deliberate, and supported at each stage" — testing assumptions under real conditions at each step before expanding to higher volumes. This applies to agentic AI deployments specifically, where the increased autonomy of AI systems amplifies the cost of architectural mistakes.

A notable challenge for governance frameworks: many organizations treat governance as a speed brake rather than a foundation. The practitioners pushed back directly on this framing, arguing that governance — clear process ownership, error observability, and standardized deployment — is what enables safe scaling, not what prevents it. The counterintuitive point: companies that invest in governance infrastructure early move faster at scale, not slower.

The article also surfaces the "Centre of Excellence" model as the organizational structure best suited to sustaining intelligent automation. Centralizing deployment standards and oversight while allowing departmental implementation is cited as the approach that keeps automation operationally viable as it expands across an enterprise.

## Key Data Points

- Conference: Intelligent Automation Conference; participants from Royal Mail, NatWest Group, Air Liquide, AXA XL
- Promise Akwaowo (Royal Mail): "If your automation engine requires constant sizing, provisioning, and babysitting, you haven't built a scalable platform; you've built a fragile service"
- Akwaowo on deployment: "progress must be gradual, deliberate, and supported at each stage"
- Gartner prediction: over 40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by 2027 due to cost, inaccuracy, and governance challenges
- MIT Technology Review Insights (500 senior IT leaders surveyed, Dec 2025): 76% of companies have at least one department with AI workflow fully in production
- Same survey: only 34% of organizations have a dedicated team for maintaining AI workflows
- Recommended organizational model: Centre of Excellence for standardizing deployment approaches
- Key diagnostic questions for scaling readiness: error observability (can you identify and understand failures?), process ownership (who owns each automated workflow?), variability (are you automating a stable process or an unstable one?)
