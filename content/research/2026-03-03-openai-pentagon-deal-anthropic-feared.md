# OpenAI's Pentagon Deal: The Compromise Anthropic Refused to Make

**Title:** OpenAI's "compromise" with the Pentagon is what Anthropic feared

**Source URL:** https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/03/02/1133850/openais-compromise-with-the-pentagon-is-what-anthropic-feared/

**Publication Date:** March 2, 2026

**Keyword Match:** Anthropic vs. US government / AI governance; AI safety and agent oversight

**Note on Cross-Day Deduplication:** The previous research file `2026-02-28-anthropic-pentagon-ai-governance-standoff.md` covered the initial standoff and Anthropic's designation as a supply chain risk. This file covers a major new development: OpenAI struck a deal with the Pentagon on February 28 — after Anthropic was banned — and MIT Technology Review's analysis reveals that OpenAI's "compromise" is substantively weaker than what Anthropic refused. This is a meaningful narrative shift: from "standoff" to "resolution and its implications."

**Summary:**
On February 28, 2026, OpenAI announced it had reached a deal with the US Department of Defense, allowing military use of its technologies in classified settings — a deal CEO Sam Altman admitted was "definitely rushed" and pursued only after the Pentagon publicly reprimanded Anthropic. MIT Technology Review's analysis argues that while OpenAI framed the deal as preserving its red lines against autonomous weapons and mass surveillance, a close reading of the published contract excerpt shows the company essentially agreed to let the Pentagon use its AI for any "lawful use," relying on existing laws rather than explicit prohibitions — precisely the arrangement Anthropic refused. The Pentagon has granted agencies six months to phase out Claude and phase in OpenAI's models, yet Claude was reportedly still used in US strikes on Iran hours after the ban was issued, underscoring the operational complexity of the swap. Meanwhile, Anthropic has signaled it will challenge the supply chain risk designation in court, and OpenAI has publicly opposed the designation as well.

**Key Data Points:**
- OpenAI announced the Pentagon deal on February 28, 2026, hours after Anthropic was designated a "supply chain risk"
- Sam Altman acknowledged the negotiations were "definitely rushed" and began only after the Pentagon's reprimand of Anthropic
- OpenAI's published contract excerpt does not give the company an "Anthropic-style, free-standing right to prohibit otherwise-lawful government use" — per Jessica Tillipman, associate dean for government procurement law studies at George Washington University
- OpenAI's agreement relies on existing laws (including a 2023 Pentagon autonomous weapons directive and the Fourth Amendment) rather than explicit contractual prohibitions
- Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's supply chain risk designation includes a ban on any contractor, supplier, or partner doing business with the military from also doing business with Anthropic — a "death blow" whose legal viability is debated
- The Pentagon has six months to phase out Claude; Claude was reportedly used in Iran strikes hours after the ban
- Anthropic has said it will sue if the supply chain risk designation is pursued
- OpenAI has also publicly opposed the designation of Anthropic as a supply chain risk

**Why It Matters:**
This story reframes the Anthropic/Pentagon saga as something more significant than a single company's standoff: it is a test of whether AI companies can actually set meaningful limits on government use of their models. OpenAI's deal reveals the structural pressure on AI companies to accept government terms — and raises serious questions for enterprise buyers about where their AI vendors' loyalty ultimately lies when government demands conflict with safety commitments. For any organization evaluating AI vendors based on their governance stances, the contrast between OpenAI's pragmatic approach and Anthropic's principled refusal is now a live, concrete case study. The story also highlights that the operational reality of AI deployment in national security settings is already far ahead of the policy frameworks meant to govern it.
