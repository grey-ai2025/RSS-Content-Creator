## Summary

Wired examines the emergent Silicon Valley thesis that the most valuable human skill in the AI age is not coding, data science, or even prompt engineering — it is "agency": the ability to identify what AI systems should work toward, delegate tasks effectively to multiple agents, and course-correct when outputs diverge from intent. Now that AI coding agents can handle most of the technical grunt work (writing, testing, debugging code), the cognitive premium has shifted to the person who decides what gets built and why. The article explores how this is reshaping hiring priorities at AI-native companies, where the ability to direct multi-agent workflows is increasingly valued over the ability to execute individual technical tasks. This parallels the broader AI-agents-as-coworkers narrative — but reframes it around what humans need to bring to that partnership.

---

## Key Data Points

- Core thesis: As AI handles execution, the scarce skill becomes goal-setting, delegation, and judgment
- Framing: "Agentic" is being applied to people as well as AI systems in Silicon Valley discourse
- Context: AI coding agents can now independently write, test, and iterate on code
- Hiring shift: AI-native companies are prioritizing workers who can direct agents over those who can execute tasks
- Parallel trend: LinkedIn's 2026 Skills on the Rise report flagged AI engineering, prompt engineering, and MLOps as top hiring priorities
- Adjacent development: Block's Jack Dorsey cited AI's capability gain — not economic pressure — as the explicit reason for 40% workforce reduction
- Wired framing: The worker who survives is the one who decides what the AI does, not the one who does what the AI now can
- Cultural resonance: "Agentic" individual = someone who acts with initiative and self-direction; Silicon Valley is adopting this as a people-selection metric

---

## Why It Matters

This is the counternarrative to the Block layoffs story — and both are true simultaneously. Yes, AI is eliminating certain roles. But the roles being created, and the roles being preserved, share a common trait: direction over execution. For LinkedIn's professional audience, this is a highly actionable framing: the question is not "will AI take my job" but "am I the person who tells AI what to do, or am I the person doing what AI now does?" The "agentic" framing also has longevity — it will persist as AI capability expands because the gap between "machine that executes" and "human who sets intent" remains real regardless of how capable the machine becomes.
