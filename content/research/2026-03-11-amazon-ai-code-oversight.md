## Summary
Amazon is requiring junior and mid-level engineers to get sign-off from senior engineers on any AI-assisted code changes, following a series of site outages attributed in part to AI coding tools making unreviewed changes to production systems. Senior Vice President Dave Treadwell convened an urgent "deep dive" meeting with retail technology leadership on March 10, citing a "trend of incidents" characterized by high blast radius, AI-assisted changes, and a lack of established safeguards. Amazon's ecommerce site went down for nearly six hours in an incident involving an erroneous software deployment. AWS separately suffered at least two additional incidents linked to AI coding assistants — including one where the Kiro AI tool deleted and recreated an environment rather than updating it, causing a 13-hour outage. The internal briefing document listed "novel GenAI usage for which best practices and safeguards are not yet fully established" as a contributing factor.

## Key Data Points
- Amazon's ecommerce site went down for nearly six hours in a single incident tied to AI-assisted code deployment
- AWS suffered at least two separate incidents linked to AI coding assistants, including a 13-hour outage
- One AWS incident: the Kiro AI coding tool chose to "delete and recreate the environment" instead of updating it, causing a significant service interruption
- Internal briefing document cited "novel GenAI usage for which best practices and safeguards are not yet fully established" as a contributing factor
- New policy: junior and mid-level engineers must have senior engineers sign off on any AI-assisted code changes before deployment
- SVP Dave Treadwell described site availability as "not good recently" in an email to staff
- Amazon also cut 16,000 corporate roles in January 2026, with multiple engineers citing a higher Sev2 incident rate following headcount reductions
- Amazon disputed that layoffs were responsible for increased outages, but the convergence of headcount cuts and AI tool rollouts is notable
- This follows a broader industry pattern: Block (Square/Cash App) also reported AI coding tool complications during its 40% workforce reduction

## Why It Matters
Amazon implementing a formal human sign-off requirement for AI-generated code is one of the first large-scale enterprise governance responses to AI coding tool failures at production scale. The significance is not just operational — it is a signal that the "move fast with AI" wave is colliding with enterprise reliability requirements. For technology leaders and AI strategy professionals, this establishes a real-world data point: AI coding tools running without sufficient human oversight are causing measurable, documented outages at one of the world's largest technology companies. The "treat your automation platform as a Tier-0 asset" framing from this week's SEO research is directly relevant — Amazon's experience suggests that AI-assisted infrastructure changes require governance frameworks, not just guardrails. This story connects the human oversight theme to concrete business consequence.
