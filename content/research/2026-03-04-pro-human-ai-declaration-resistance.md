## Summary

In early January 2026, approximately 90 political, religious, labor, and community leaders gathered in a New Orleans Marriott for a secret conference organized by the Future of Life Institute (FLI) — a leading AI safety organization. The meeting was deliberately kept free of tech industry representatives; attendees included AFL-CIO leaders, the American Federation of Teachers, the Screen Writers Guild, the Progressive Democrats of America, the Congress of Christian Leaders, conservative think tanks, and individual signatories ranging from Ralph Nader to Steve Bannon. On March 4, FLI published the result: the "Pro-Human AI Declaration," a five-principle document centered on keeping AI development human-centered, preventing concentration of power, protecting children and families, and preserving human agency. The document has since been signed by a remarkable breadth of civic organizations and individuals spanning the entire political spectrum. The declaration explicitly responds to what organizers describe as a vacuum created by the Trump administration's AI deregulation posture, asserting that "if the government won't do it, then the people have to force the government to do it."

## Key Data Points

- Organized by **Max Tegmark**, MIT professor and co-founder of the Future of Life Institute (TIME 100 AI list)
- Approximately **90 attendees** from across the political spectrum; meeting was under **Chatham House Rules**
- Signatories include: **AFL-CIO Tech Institute, American Federation of Teachers, Screen Writers Guild, G20 Interfaith Forum Association, Congress of Christian Leaders, Progressive Democrats of America, Institute for Family Studies, Parents RISE!, SAG-AFTRA**
- Individual signatories include: **Ralph Nader, Randi Weingarten, Meredith Whittaker (Signal Foundation), Glenn Beck (The Blaze), Steve Bannon (War Room), Sir Richard Branson, Susan Rice (former NSA)**
- The **least popular principle** in the declaration — AI must not create monopolies — still received **94% approval** from attendees
- FLI poll (Tavern Research, February 2026): the **worst-performing principle got 69% support** from general voters; the best-performing principle got **80% support**
- The 5 declaration principles focus on: human agency, preventing power concentration, protecting children/families/communities, opposing AI legal personhood, opposing autonomous lethal weapons
- **No tech company representatives were invited** — a deliberate design choice to prevent corporate interests from dominating the agenda
- Contrast with 2017 Asilomar AI Principles: signed by Sam Altman, Elon Musk, Demis Hassabis — the same people the 2026 declaration is implicitly opposing
- FLI is running a campaign called **"Protect What's Human"** — but as a 501(c)3, cannot endorse midterm candidates

## Why It Matters

The Pro-Human AI Declaration represents the first serious attempt to build a durable cross-ideological civil society coalition against unchecked AI development — and it succeeded in getting Steve Bannon and the AFL-CIO to sign the same document. For enterprise professionals, this is the leading indicator of the regulatory environment that will crystallize over the next 2-3 years. When labor unions, religious organizations, and conservative think tanks align on the same AI guardrail principles, legislation follows. Organizations that have not yet thought through their AI governance stance — particularly on autonomous decision-making, workforce displacement, and child safety — should treat this declaration as a preview of future regulatory requirements, not a fringe political statement. The absence of any tech company representatives is itself a signal: civil society has stopped waiting for industry self-regulation.
