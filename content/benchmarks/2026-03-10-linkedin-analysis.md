## Pattern Analysis

### Hook Patterns
- **Most common (4/14): Announcement/news-jack** — Leading with breaking news or product launches. Example: "That was quick! OpenAI has reportedly started rolling out..."
- **Second (3/14): Contrarian/reframe** — Challenging conventional thinking. Example: "The hardest AI shift isn't being more efficient..."
- **Third (3/14): Stat/social proof** — Opening with a number that creates a curiosity gap. Example: "I published this article 24 hours ago. It has over 30 million views."
- **Observation:** Pure question hooks were absent from the top performers. The highest-engagement hooks either stated a strong position or led with a surprising data point.

### Format Distribution
- Text with link/article: 29%
- Video (short, under 3 min): 29%
- Text with image: 21%
- Text-only: 7%
- Text with carousel: 7%
- Text with linked long-form article (LinkedIn Pulse): 7%

### Topic Themes
1. **AI agents and agentic workflows** — Courses on building agents, agent design patterns, AI hiring humans, Copilot agents (5 posts)
2. **AI capability step-changes and predictions** — Cost collapses, year-ahead outlooks, "Something Big Is Happening" (4 posts)
3. **Practical AI tools and workflows** — Voice AI, Excel COPILOT(), guides on which AIs to use (3 posts)
4. **AI's impact on work and education** — Vibefounding, outputs vs inputs, AI in MBA programs (2 posts)

### Structural Patterns
- **Numbered lists dominate:** 9 of 14 top posts use numbered points (observations, steps, features, evidence signals). Lists of 3-4 items perform best.
- **Framework posts outperform opinion posts:** Posts that name a framework or mental model (four agentic design patterns, outputs-over-inputs, full-stack control) generate higher comment engagement because they give readers vocabulary to use.
- **Short post + long-form link is a proven combo:** 4 of 14 top posts use a brief LinkedIn caption (1-3 sentences) that links to a comprehensive article or course page. The LinkedIn post sells the click; the linked content delivers the depth.
- **Video sweet spot is under 3 minutes:** All video posts that performed well were under 3 minutes. Nadella's 20-second Excel demo massively outperformed longer-form content.

### Length & Engagement Correlation
- **Short posts (1-3 sentences + media) dominate at the very top:** The two highest-engagement posts (Nadella's Excel demo at 72K likes, Shumer's virality meta-post at 10.7K) were extremely brief.
- **Medium-length posts (150-300 words) are the sweet spot for thought leadership:** Miller's and Mollick's best-performing posts fall here — long enough for substance, short enough to read without expanding.
- **Long text-only posts can work but require a strong hook and numbered structure:** Mollick's "vibefounding" post (3.4K likes) succeeded at length because the neologism hook and numbered observations carried it.
- **The linked article model bypasses length constraints:** Ng, Nadella, and Mollick all use LinkedIn as a teaser for longer content hosted elsewhere, getting both engagement and depth.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Lead with a number or a coined term, not a question.** The highest-performing hooks used stats ("72,222 likes on a 20-second video"), cost collapses ("$250k to a few bucks"), or neologisms ("vibefounding"). Zero top posts opened with a question.

2. **Name your framework.** Posts that introduced a named concept (agentic design patterns, full-stack control, outputs-over-inputs, vibefounding) generated disproportionate comments because readers adopt and repeat the vocabulary. Give your thesis a label.

3. **Use the short-teaser-plus-linked-depth model.** Write 1-3 punchy sentences on LinkedIn, then link to the full analysis. This respects the scroll while rewarding the click. Works especially well for research-backed content.

4. **Keep video under 60 seconds for maximum reach.** Nadella's 20-second Excel demo outperformed every other post in this dataset by 3x+. Short video demos of tangible tools beat talking-head thought leadership. Show, don't tell.

5. **Structure with 3-4 numbered points.** Numbered observations, steps, or features are the dominant structure across all influencers. Four items is the sweet spot — enough to feel substantive, few enough to remember. Each point should be independently valuable (scannable, not sequential).
