## Pattern Analysis

### Hook Patterns

1. **Contrarian take** — the dominant hook style across all top performers. Most common form: "Despite what people believe..." / "X might not be the skill that matters" / "You don't need a co-founder anymore". Works because LinkedIn's algorithm rewards comment velocity, and contrarian claims reliably generate disagree-comments that inflate reach. Examples: Mollick's "bright line" post, Miller's "prompting is not the skill", Ng's "AI bubble" reframe.

2. **Provocative metaphor** — second most common. A concrete, unexpected image collapses a complex idea into a single memorable frame. Examples: Shumer's "Covid blindside" mapped to AI, Miller's "Swiss Army knife to build a house", Beliunas's "writing code by hand in 2026". The metaphor does not need to be airtight — it needs to be vivid enough to survive a forward or a repost.

3. **News-jack + "preview of"** — Schick and Beliunas's primary mode. Takes a breaking news item (China 5-year plan, Pentagon-Anthropic standoff, Block CEO statement) and frames it as evidence of a larger structural claim. The news provides credibility; the interpretation provides shareability. Opens with the event, lands on the implication.

4. **Self-aware meta-observation** — Mollick's signature. Posts that observe the mechanics of their own virality ("glance at the comments") or that name the reader's own cognitive response ("wish casting"). Generates recursive engagement — readers engage to prove or disprove the observation.

### Format Distribution

- Text-only: ~67%
- Carousel/list (tool breakdowns, numbered frameworks): ~20%
- Long-form article (LinkedIn Pulse): ~7%
- Text + external link: ~6%

Text-only dominates because LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses reach for posts with external links. Carousels appear specifically for tool/resource posts where the list IS the value. Long-form articles only appear when the author has established platform authority and the topic has universal professional stakes.

### Topic Themes

1. **AI capability step-change** — the single most engaging topic area. Posts claiming AI has crossed a threshold (Shumer's essay, Mollick's "bright line", Nadella's "spectacle to substance") consistently generate the highest comment counts because they force readers to self-locate relative to the claim.

2. **AI job and skill displacement** — what skills survive, which jobs are at risk, what to retrain on. Generates both agreement from AI enthusiasts and defensive pushback from the threatened — both drive algorithmic reach. Miller's "prompting is not the skill", Shumer's job displacement section, Beliunas's "co-founder replaced by AI stack".

3. **Geopolitics of AI** — Schick's core territory. China policy, Pentagon-Anthropic, sovereign AI strategy. Attracts a distinct, highly engaged professional audience (policy, defence, enterprise) that comments with unusual length and seriousness.

4. **AI investment and bubble dynamics** — Andrew Ng's primary mode. Training-layer vs. application-layer distinction, where the real risk is, which bets are irrational. Attracts investors and CFOs who rarely engage but post high-quality comments that signal prestige.

5. **Practical AI tools and workflows** — Miller and Beliunas's core territory. Tool-by-task breakdowns, AI stacks for founders, specific model recommendations. Generates saves and shares more than comments — still high-value engagement because shares contribute to LinkedIn reach scoring.

### Structural Patterns

- **The three-pillar framework** (Nadella, Ng) works for credibility-first audiences (enterprise, C-suite). Each pillar needs a name, not just a number. "Scaffolding for human potential / models to systems / deliberate diffusion" is more shareable than "Point 1, Point 2, Point 3".
- **The binary split** (old model vs. new model) is the fastest-reading structure on LinkedIn. It collapses nuance into a choice, which the reader immediately takes a side on. Examples: co-founder vs. AI stack; prompting vs. context engineering; spectacle vs. substance.
- **The open loop opener** ("A developer writing code by hand in 2026 looks increasingly like...") forces the reader to complete the sentence mentally before continuing. This is the single most reliable first-sentence technique for increasing scroll-stopping rate.
- **Named eras and coined terms** ("Silicon Realism", "model overhang", "intelligence explosion") make readers feel they are participating in naming history. They share the term, not just the post — but the post gets the credit.

### Length and Engagement Correlation

- **Short posts (<100 words)** perform best for pure engagement rate (likes per impression), particularly when the hook is a single striking observation or metaphor. Mollick's meta-observation posts and Beliunas's one-liners exemplify this. Best for driving shares.
- **Long-form (300+ words or full articles)** generates the highest absolute comment volume and drives follow-on debate. Shumer's essay (10,792 likes, 1,216 comments) is the outlier case. Long-form only wins when the author has established platform authority and the topic has universal professional stakes.
- **Medium posts (100–300 words)** are the workhorse format — predictable engagement, sustainable cadence, lower execution risk. The majority of top performers across all 8 influencers cluster here.
- **Carousels** outperform text-only for save and share signals (tool lists, frameworks) but underperform for raw comment count. Best format for content intended to be bookmarked rather than debated.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Lead every post with an open loop or a direct binary.** The two highest-performing opening structures are "X looks increasingly like..." (forces mental completion before the punchline) and "X might not be the skill/thing/company you think it is" (forces re-evaluation of an existing belief). Never open with context-setting — context belongs in sentence two or three.

2. **Mint one coined phrase per post.** Schick's "Silicon Realism", Nadella's "model overhang", Ng's "application-layer underinvestment" all give readers language they can deploy in their next meeting or post. Posts that supply precise language for a real phenomenon get shared as intellectual currency. Grey AI posts should coin one phrase per piece — not jargon, but a label for something real that currently has no name.

3. **Anchor every abstract claim to a specific named company or number.** "AI is transforming fintech" is forgettable. "Block is rebuilding from scratch around AI" is a post. Specificity is the functional difference between a take and a trend piece — readers trust the specific claim and share it as evidence.

4. **When news-jacking, always add the structural implication sentence.** Schick's pattern: event (China 5-year plan) + implication (AI is now a national mobilisation asset, not a market commodity). The event makes the post timely; the implication makes it shareable. Posts that stop at the event are news. Posts that add the implication are analysis. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards analysis with longer dwell time.

5. **Use carousel format only when the list IS the value and the reader will want to save it.** Tool breakdowns, step-by-step frameworks, model comparison tables. Never use a carousel to make a single argument that could be made in two paragraphs — it adds friction without adding information. Text-only is the right default for Grey AI's analytical voice; carousels are reserved for reference content designed to be bookmarked.
