# Federal Judge Halts Perplexity's AI Shopping Agents on Amazon — The First Major Legal Test of Agentic Authorization

**Title:** Judge orders Perplexity to stop AI agents from shopping on Amazon

**Source URL:** https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/892401/amazon-perplexity-ai-shopping-agent-court-order

**Publication Date:** March 10, 2026

**Keyword Match:** AI agent security, AI agents, AI governance

**Summary:**
A federal judge has issued a preliminary injunction blocking Perplexity's AI browser Comet from placing Amazon orders on users' behalf, finding that Amazon provided "strong evidence" that the AI agents accessed the marketplace "without authorization." The ruling marks the first significant legal precedent on the boundaries of agentic AI commerce — specifically, what permissions an AI agent must have before it can act on behalf of a user within third-party platforms. Amazon sued Perplexity in November 2025, alleging that Comet's agentic shopping feature violated computer fraud and abuse laws, and that Perplexity attempted to "conceal" its activity by misrepresenting Comet as Google Chrome. Under the injunction, Perplexity must also destroy any Amazon data it may have collected.

**Key Data Points:**
- US District Judge Maxine Chesney ruled Amazon provided "strong evidence" of unauthorized access by Perplexity's Comet browser
- Amazon sued Perplexity in November 2025 after allegedly asking the company to stop its agentic shopping feature multiple times
- Amazon alleged Perplexity violated computer fraud and abuse laws by "intruding" into its marketplace and user accounts
- Perplexity allegedly "concealed" its AI agent activity by misrepresenting Comet as Google Chrome
- Under the injunction: Perplexity must not access Amazon via AI agents AND must destroy data already obtained; Perplexity has 7 days to comply or appeal
- Amazon statement: the ruling "will prevent Perplexity's unauthorized access to the Amazon store"
- Perplexity statement: the startup "will continue to fight for the right of internet users to choose whatever AI they want"
- The case directly intersects with the broader OpenClaw/agentic web moment: as AI agents proliferate, authorization frameworks are being tested in real time

**Why It Matters:**
This ruling draws the first legal boundary around what AI agents are permitted to do — and establishes that "acting on a user's behalf" does not automatically confer authorization to access third-party platforms. For enterprise architects deploying agentic AI, for legal teams advising on AI liability, and for product teams building autonomous systems that interact with external services, this case is a foundational data point. The authorization question — who decides what an AI agent is allowed to do, and how that permission is verified — is now being answered by courts, not just by companies. That shift will shape how agentic AI gets deployed at scale.

**Related Themes:** AI agent security, AI agents, AI governance, agentic commerce, legal precedent, Perplexity, Amazon, computer fraud, authorization frameworks, enterprise AI risk
